Cosmologies of Asylum: An Introductory Essay
Carlota Mir

Some (art) projects can only come to be if there is friendship’.
Nikos Papastergiadis, On Art and Friendship, 2020

1. Massaging the Asylum System as Lumbung Practice: Context

Curated by Trampoline House Artistic Team members Carlota Mir and Sara Alberani, Massaging the Asylum System is a collaboration between two collectives and Lumbung members* at documenta fifteen: Copenhagen-based refugee justice centre Trampoline House (TH) and UK-based neurodiverse collective Project Art Works (PAW). Initiated by Mir and Alberani and funded with money from the Lumbung pot after a collective decision-making process, the collaboration emerged from the shared affinities between both organisations that were discovered during the Lumbung process. It crystallised in two series of public workshops between Copenhagen and Kassel: Massaging the Asylum System - A Collective Exploration, and ‘Massaging the Asylum System: Creative Strategies of Care.

In the workshops, we merged the methods from both organisations to create spaces for dialogue and friendship and look at the asylum system in Denmark. In this process of sharing conversations and strategies, visualising systems of care and control, repositioning ourselves and our communities in them, and opening processes up to publics, a specific artistic tool for system visualisation became central: created by PAW artist and co-founder Kate Adams and developed throughout the Lumbung process, Cosmologies of Care are large-scale, participatory, circular drawings that help visualise the social systems of care that people with complex support needs in the UK and their families must go through to get their needs met. They also make care visible as a vital form of labour, and help visualise deeper human aspirations - from choice in who to spend time with to living close to the sea - which are often overlooked and neglected in the lives of people with disabilities, and, as we came to realise, people who are marginalised in other ways.

Image: Kate Adams, Cosmologies of Care - Drawing III - Navigating Systems, 2021. Photo: author

The translation of the cosmologies into the context of asylum underlined their value as an endlessly translatable and adaptable practice that puts people in the centre, helps raise self awareness, visualises infrastructures, trauma and desires, and articulates individual thoughts and feelings as part of larger collective constellations. Here, translation, circulation, and circularity emerge as ongoing motion - artistic practices abandon rigid, individual and authorial forms to shift shape, transform and merge, to tell stories of different communities, make the invisible visible, and build alliances, also building solidarity and bridges among locales and within the exhibition space of documenta fifteen, which became embodied otherwise.

Entitled Cosmologies of Asylum as a reference to both PAW’s artistic methodologies and Trampoline House’s community, this harvest* presents the fruits of a year-long collective conversation between the artistic teams and the larger communities which make up both organisations and whose voices resonate here. Beginning with how the Cosmologies first emerged as individual tools for self-awareness, the harvest follow the drawings as they move through the emotional and physical spaces of Project Art Works, Trampoline House and documenta fifteen. Through movement, translations and shifts, the cosmologies become strong tools that help not only detect malaise, navigate adversarial systems, express anger, and make visible the invisible: they also become essential to voice desires, aspirations and utopias, bringing more joyful futures into hopeful perspective.

We were able to propose, initiate and develop this collaboration thanks to the openness of our teams and communities, the support of the Lumbung inter-lokal* network and its practices of resource sharing. Massaging the Asylum System couldn’t have happened without the support of Trampoline House co-founder and curator Tone Olaf Nielsen and the close collaboration of Project Art Works artists Kate Adams, Tim Corrigan, and Martin Swan. It also couldn’t have happened without the input of people in Trampoline House and the support of the Project Art Works community. In short, this is a collective venture.

The work took shape as a practice of Lumbung* values: friendship, generosity, resource sharing, art as life, and regeneration. Curators ruangrupa had initially proposed the idea of Lumbung - an Indonesian rice barn - as a collectively governed pot of surplus resources.

Our collaboration was born out of the collective pot*, a collectively governed economic resource that worked as a way to speculate artistically on how to build and maintain such a common structure over time. It was unique in its alliance of two lumbung members experimentally bringing together methods that were translated into proposals for Kassel.

Image: Collective pot + Lumbung members, diagram. Carlota Mir

When both of our collectives were invited to participate in documenta fifteen as a Lumbung member, one of ruangrupa's statements was: 'Art is rooted in life. Instead of commissioning new art works, we asked artists and collectives to keep doing what they do in their localities while harvesting it.’ One of the ways that we began building a relationship was the realisation that both of us were, literally, doing just that in our proposals to documenta: centring our localities, aiming to translate our practices into the space of the exhibition by summoning real lives and stories - bodies, emotions, trauma, and joy - and using art as a tool to create public conversations about hard and deeply unglamourous topics: asylum and disability. Our work centred on solidarity practices and building safer spaces where individual and collective needs can be negotiated and stories shared, but always in relationship to larger systems and their oppressions, spreading social awareness through creative, clear and direct action.

A massage is a soft, directed action applied to the body that, through intimate connection and applying pressure, aims to relieve pain. A practice that must involve two or more people, the massage encourages gentle healing and invites openness and the softening of rigid tissues. In this spirit, our collaboration sought to create a soft space for meeting, self-awareness and exchange, which was (trans)locally rooted but which could eventually translate into the space of Kassel, reaching wider publics and engaging unaware passers-by in our political struggles and the topics concerning our work, from an approach where they would also have to situate themselves and take on an active role. Merging our practices in spirit and materiality, our work became a temporary coalition in the Lumbung network: together, we looked at the ambiguous social systems of care and control that operate on neurodivergent and migrant communities alike, how to navigate them, and what the role of art in that conversation could be.

Combining our situated knowledge, strategies and methods, we eventually chose to focus on the cosmologies and translate them to the context of asylum in Denmark through a series of collaborative workshops that took place between Trampoline House and Kassel: we explored alternative ways to ‘massage’ the system, exercise agency and build solidarity practices that, while recognising differences, are essentially exercises in seeing, supporting and embracing one another. Using art to create visibility is particularly necessary in the case of communities who are marginalised within the already marginalised: rejected asylum seekers, people with profound learning disabilities and complex support needs; these are all collectives for whom invisibility is a daily reality.

Circulating beyond Trampoline House in Copenhagen, traces of the first series of workshops were part of the installations of both organisations in Kassel, paving the way for the second series, which involved a public audience and using the collective imagination to re-imagine asylum together. Through shared affect, time and joint creative action, our aim was to massage the rigid and inhumane asylum system - yes, massage, like a real massage - so that it could become softer and more humane.

2. From ‘Where is the Art?’ to documenta: on Common Thinking, Friendship and Caring Action

Our artistic research began by a spontaneous process of alliance and affiliation that was born out of the virtual Lumbung Inter-Lokal assemblies between the 14 core collectives - or lumbung members - leading up to documenta fifteen, which were initiated online in late 2020, with Project Art Works joining in early 2021. The questions we posed and practised in the lumbung inter-lokal network during the months leading up to the exhibition informed our collaboration: what does it mean to be locally and globally rooted today, and what potential does locality currently hold? How can we share knowledge and resources among locales? How should our artistic practices use space to redefine our relationship with the public? How can we consider regenerative/collective economies? How can we translate our practices into the space of Kassel sustainably, generatively and non-extractively?

Out of the different Lumbung groups, there was a particular research group that became the base of the collaboration: the Where is the Art?* research group, which both Trampoline House and Project Art Works joined, as we both felt that this sarcastic question resonated with the way our practices were perceived, but also with our stance towards artistic practice - putting people before artworks. We began talking to one another around questions such as: what is art without objects? Which artwork can be an example in a way that can be tested in different cultures and localities? How do we make art in a place that doesn’t perceive this practice as art?

We both felt strongly that in our practice, ‘the art’ emerged in conversations and processes of exchange and collaboration, rather than artefacts, which if they do happen at all, are traces of the actual artwork. To quote artist Tania Bruguera, who has previously participated in Trampoline House’s artistic programme was also part of documenta fifteen through INSTAR, ‘art should not be an event, but a culture of implementation’. Together, we eventually set out to explore our alliance within the larger context of interlocal exchanges between practices, artists and collectives, but with specific resonances in our practices and a particular translation of methodologies that made the process both rare and unique.

Image: Harvest from collective zoom chat, ‘Where is the Art?’, Majelis Akbar public sessions for the Lumbung network, 2022.

Our conversations also showed that this direct, caring way of working with community, where artworks become just a tool or a trace for something much more important, and where human rights are put in the centre, is central to both organisations. In this sense, the role of art as a tool to imagine reality otherwise, for reworlding, for rethinking relationships, beyond merely fixing, or maintaining, what the system has destroyed or discarded, is central to both Project Art Works and Trampoline House: there is a common appetite for dismantling established social models through the exercise of individual and collective agency and the intention to practise a set of divergent logics and tools in a sustained way.

In our exchange, very clear parallels emerged, especially regarding the way that our communities - neurodivergent people and refugees - are treated as improductive flesh or ‘surplus’ in society, - to borrow a term from the book Health Communism,4 which mentions both the asylum system and disabled communities as specific government-managed carceral-socio-medical systems of ‘care’ that produce profitable marginalisation through discipline and systemic/legal processes of categorisation that make people vulnerable.5 Concepts such as ‘social murder’ (Engels), ‘slow death’ (Lauren Berlant) ‘social death’ (Orlando Patterson/Nicholas Mirzoeff), ‘bare life’ (Giorgio Agamben), or ‘the chain’ (Shakira Kasigwa Mukamusoni-Trampoline House) illustrate the ways in which care is exercised as control upon certain collectives deemed ‘undesirable’ inside Western capitalist societies. It is the “slow violence of the everyday”, as Lauren Berlant puts it, through which our governments’ necropolitics allow for the subsistence of marginalised bodies.

Navigating both the asylum system in Denmark and social care systems in the UK, it is easy to get stuck in places where the body might be fed and cared for, but the spirit is left to waste. For instance, in Sjaelsmark, a remote Danish camp for rejected asylum seekers which we visited upon invitation by fellow Trampoline House Artistic Team member Christine,6 people live in bare rooms that resemble prison cells with random roommates, they can’t decorate their rooms or cook their own food, they describe the food that they have to eat as ‘inedible’, and every night, they are locked in every day at 10 pm. While in Denmark this responds to a deliberate government strategy to make people’s mental health waste away in camps over the years, thus deterring them from seeking asylum - a tactic that Nicholas Mirzoeff has termed ‘social death’,7 in the UK we find that dehumanisation can still be a consequence of well-meaning, yet still partly inadequate social care. According to scholar Jill Bradshaw, despite the changes from institutionalised to communal living for learning disabled people in the UK, which are part of a larger strategy for the individualisation of care based on catering for a person’s needs as opposed to homogenising an entire social group, ‘it is of course possible to transfer many of the negative features of institutional living to life in a community house: rigid routines, lack of choice, little opportunity to participate in activities both in and out of the home, no control over where you live or with whom, limited contact with the wider community…8 In other words, invisibility and dehumanisation are still recurrent realities in social care. This might also be partly due to the fact that social care services in the UK have suffered a comprehensive process of devaluation, de-funding and privatisation in the last decades.9

Basing ourselves on experience rather than theory, we arrived at the same point through conversation: this shared perspective by our communities allowed us to reflect on how the capitalist organisation of care produces and reproduces our societies’ necropolitical assumptions. As Pascale Molinier writes, ‘care is the neuralgic conflict zone’.10 What is saved and what is not, on whom the burden of care work falls and on whom it does not, who is allowed to provide care for others and who remains a victim, who lives and who only affords to survive, are decisions at the heart of every society, historically formalised by different models of care labour organisation.11 As a consequence of that, social systems of care, which are often adversarial, hard to navigate, and have deteriorated enormously in the last decades, often fail to detect, let alone attend to an individual’s human needs and aspirations. Because the vulnerability of our communities appears to be, to a large extent, system fabricated, it becomes a field of intervention: care and control emerge as a shifting dichotomy, causing our work to move between two poles: from lack (of proper care) and experience (of being controlled) to freedom to (care)/freedom from (control). In the cosmologies, freedom emerges first as speculative futurity, and eventually as a potentially tangible outcome: a better asylum system.

In the collaboration process, frictions and antagonisms also emerged, from Trampoline House house members feeling a lack of connection with what was perceived as a white, ‘foreign body’, to antagonising positions adopted by some people - especially young, educated men with refugee backgrounds - for whom joint action seemed to imply an equation of both communities. In other words - the fear of being equated with what they perceived as a weaker Other - and thus re-marginalised - emerged in parts of the process, attesting to the reality that self-perceived hierarchies also exist within different marginalised groups, particularly in regards to gender, class or ability. In these moments, it was always important to keep language accessible, to be very transparent, and to underline and uphold our situated positions and differences, so as to invite non-judgemental openness and mutual understanding.

These intersections had other tangible outcomes: it is not coincidental that there were no disabled or neurodivergent refugees at Trampoline House: people with disabilities, old age and complex support needs in the asylum system are kept in camps in Jutland (another island three hours north-west of Copenhagen) in conditions of severe isolation - and that is if they make it to Denmark at all, as disabled people are much less likely to migrate in the first place.12 Sadly, through the years, Trampoline House has not had the means to bring people from Jutland into the House. In the individual cases in the house where disability is a result of the physical trauma suffered during the migration journey, we didn’t succeed in finding an appropriate way to discuss the topic in a way that the story could move from the personal to the systemic without making the person uncomfortable. Instead, we chose to focus on using PAW’s methodology, rather than the thematic intersection of disability and migration, to read the asylum system together and focus on aspirations for freedom through ‘massaging’ said system.

Caring, in this context, means struggling, devoting energy and time to transform unequal power relations.13 In this sense, I think it’s important to understand failures not as a tragedy, but as opportunities for reflection. Accepting the partial transformative power of caring practices is reading these experimentations as material struggles that ask us to “stay in touch with the problem”, as Donna Haraway states. In other words: when rehearsing critical caring tools, we shouldn’t place ourselves in a scenario of wrong vs right. Rather, we can ask ourselves: what can we do differently next time? How can we engage people better? How can we create alliances without making people feel that their identities are alienated or without making them feel like their differences are threatened? But also - how can we question hierarchies of oppression carefully and not be blind to the ways in which oppressions do intersect? In this sense, in the May workshops, we were able to use the cosmologies to analyse the differences between the ways that refugees from Ukraine were being given asylum in quick, effective, humane ways, in comparison to people from other countries who wait years and years on average. We did this by listening to people who had had these very different experiences on the spot: the experience brought about feelings of rage by some, but also respect and solidarity. In the September workshops, however, where the target participants had shifted to the documenta audience, intersections and alliances emerged without frictions. This time, it was the PAW community - the ones who had been able to travel to Kassel - that spontaneously joined the workshops and showed empathy and interest to contribute to the process of collectively imagining a better asylum system.

There were also other questions of an ethical nature, which touched upon both artistic teams in different ways throughout the entire process: how can we remain accountable when we represent communities that can’t represent themselves in the context of a big art exhibition - either because they are not allowed to be there physically (in the case of asylum seekers), because they don’t have an in-depth understanding of the context of an international art biennial (usually down to either education or previous experience) or because they don’t communicate with verbal language (in the case of some neurominorities)? As much as the aim of both practices is for people to represent themselves on their own terms, what are the ethical implications of those limitations in representation in this context? But also, what are the implications of showing art by people who don’t necessarily consider themselves as artists in a global circuit like documenta fifteen? And how does the ambiguity, and inevitable capitalisation processes that take place in the artistic realm impact grassroots participation and practice? In the face of these questions, I found that there was always a shared willingness to open up, be vulnerable and candid, and bring the questions and issues to the forefront, as opposed to shoving them under the carpet.

Reflecting on our collaboration and ‘temporary coalition’, as I have called it before, researcher Anne K. Petersen recently wrote that we had ’[taken] art’s visual and political agency seriously, casting imagination and its visual materializations as a potential ‘strong card’ in transformative politics.’14 Perhaps that is true. One thing is for sure, and that is the feeling of a jointly lived experience. Throughout the 100 days, we were in one another’s spaces, events, and things, drew new cosmologies, took portraits and developed solar prints, rushed through production lists, presented our collaboration in public events in Kassel with a collective sense of unease and vulnerability as we stepped out of our respective comfort zones, shared dinners and printers that got lost in mystery Kassel production departments, went dancing after hours to raves and sat by beautiful lakes in Kassel to see the sunrise. Together, we experienced documenta fifteen as a bittersweet experience of censorship, of joy and tears, of togetherness, sharing a sense of direction and survival in the face of hardship and the constant threat of closure over the summer of 2022, while proposing radically different forms of embodying artistic practice and being in an exhibition space. Ultimately, our process allowed us to learn to question, from a new angle, who art is for and what it is for.

While documenta fifteen is over, our practice and relationships are not: a parallel publication (featured in the homepage drawing under the visÀvis icon) that centres on the last iteration of the programme (Massaging the Asylum Systems: Creative Strategies of Care) is currently in the making. Co-produced with our independent Trampoline House publisher visÀvis and the Italian Council, it will be launched in Autumn 2023. A full harvest of Massaging the Asylum System will hopefully be going to print with Lumbung Books soon, giving way to new translations and adventures with the Lumbung Press* in Barcelona. Our encounters have already given birth to new alliances and relationships inside our different localities, in the form of translocally informed, locally rooted projects that use art to raise awareness and create alliances between different marginalised groups. At a time of deep political unrest in our various locales, we also simply seek to preserve the community bonds we created through writing, talking, and sharing work, time and physical space when possible.

3. On Trampoline House & Project Art Works at documenta fifteen

Though very different in aesthetic, character and scope, our proposals for documenta fifteen also had something in common: our installations and their public activations were thoughtful propositions where art was a vehicle for having difficult conversations about the lives of our communities. These were told in the collective first person, placed within the larger framework of human rights, and situating agency and direct action as a focus point.

Located in the Hübner building, Trampoline House’s Castle in Kassel consisted of a warm, cosy, Scandinavian-style living room set up inside a chalk circle - a weak, imaginary construction that demarcated the house as a protected space, decidedly yet precariously shielding people from the hostility facing migrants in Denmark.

Image: Wall text, documenta fifteen: installation view, Trampoline House, 2022, Hübner area, Kassel, June 15, 2022, photo: Frank Sperling.
Image: documenta fifteen: installation view, Trampoline House, 2022, Hübner area, Kassel, June 15, 2022, photo: Frank Sperling.

On the outside: a wall, reading - THIS IS WHAT YOU FACE WHEN SEEKING ASYLUM IN DENMARK - listed an endless trail of dehumanising protocols by the Danish government, while the combination of space and artworks - film and sound pieces, animation, a visit book where visitors could write and share their thoughts and feelings, zines, a fashion collection, masks, protest signs - invited the viewer to walk around, sit down, read and watch TV and come into contact and direct empathy with the reality of Danish - and by extension European - migration policies and their impact on people’s dignity. Making visible the fact that our wider community could not be physically present in Kassel because of their legal status, our Artistic Team, made up of 14 people with mixed refugee and non-refugee backgrounds, put together a programme for the 100 days. We chose to focus on very different site activations: a fashion show, public talks, poetry readings, public drawing sessions and performances on the migrant question and its political and ethical underpinnings, always placing migrant and refugee knowledge, embodiment and experience at the centre, referring back to the house for feedback and inviting what we call ‘deprogramming’ - a collective questioning of one’s own assumptions, privileges and positions through shared vulnerability and affect in the space of the House, whose spirit we had aimed to translate into our living room at Hübner.

For its part, Project Art Works’s supported studio practice for neurodivergent and disabled artists, which spans 20 years, radiated out of the Fredericianum into multiple sites across the city, along with collaborations with audiences, carers, neurodivergent people, and artists. Their main installation space in the Fridericianum was a direct proposition to have a conversation around disability, neurodivergence and complex needs through the coming together of three main elements: the open archive - artworks & film - of artists from the PAW collective in Hastings; the Cosmologies of Care, with public drawing sessions and circles progressively filling up the space and the telling the stories of people, carers and communities, and an immersive workshop set-up that worked as a reminder of PAW’s core engagement with neurodivergent people through building relationships and sustained supported studio practice. In the space, translations of the cosmologies from our joint workshops in Copenhagen also emerged over the weeks, speaking about freedom to German-speaking audiences in their mother tongue.

4. Glossary

*Collective Pot. The Collective Pot is a collectively governed and economic infrastructure that was proposed by ruangrupa to all fourteen Lumbung members. Thanks to the Collective Pot, projects benefiting the lumbung community were collectively approved and funded. Its aims were:
1. To develop economic models that liberate us from funding cycles and allow us to become more sustainable and independent.
2. To use the documenta period and platform to start building our relations and collaborations in the lumbung, to start connecting our ecosystems beyond the core of the organisations - collectives.
3. To experiment with our interdependency and values: if an individual lumbung member is in need, how can we support them through non-monetary and monetary resources in the collective rice barn?

Image: Collective pot + Lumbung members, diagram. Carlota Mir

*Cosmologies of Care. Cosmologies of Care are large-scale, participatory, circular drawings that help visualise the social systems of care that people with complex support needs in the UK must go through to get their needs met and live fulfilling lives. They were created by Project Art Works artist Kate Adams and became central pieces in PAW’s documenta fifteen methodologies.

*Harvest. As a Lumbung practice, harvest refers to artistic recordings of discussions and meetings. Harvesters listen, reflect, and depict this process from their own perspectives, forms, and artistic practices. Harvests can be humorous, poetic, or candid. They can take the shape of a sticky note, a written story, drawing, film, sound piece, or meme. Harvesting can be seen as a way of collective writing that enables continuous collective learning, from different sensory experiences. Harvests are made to share what is being discussed with absent members and the general public and they are present throughout the entire process of documenta fifteen, and beyond.

*Lumbung. Lumbung is an Indonesian rice barn where surplus rice is stored for whomever in the community needs it. Lumbung as a collectively-governed architecture for the storage of food serves a community’s long-term wellbeing through communal resources and mutual care, and it is organised around a set of shared values, collective rituals, and organisational principles. Ruangrupa translates and continues this tradition of sharing within our own practice. As an artistic and economic model, Lumbung is practised alongside its values of collectivity, generosity, humour, trust, independence, curiosity, endurance, regeneration, transparency, sufficiency, and connectivity between a multiplicity of locales, rendering them planetary as a result.

*Lumbung Inter-lokal. Lumbung inter-lokal refers to the international network of lumbung members. The term inter-local describes the intertwining of their local practices as well as their international dissemination via the network.

*Lumbung Members. Lumbung members are community-oriented collectives, organisations and institutions invited by ruangrupa and the artistic team to work together to further develop the idea and practice of lumbung . Together they form an interdisciplinary platform for contemporary art that aims to have an impact beyond the 2022 exhibition. The international network of 14 lumbung members is called lumbung inter-lokal. In addition to working together on the fundamental ideas and questions of documenta fifteen, the lumbung members – as well as the lumbung artists – presented their own projects in the exhibition. They also invited other participants with whom to create their artistic works.

*Lumbung Press. Lumbung Press is a project by lumbung members, lumbung artists and the documenta fifteen artistic team. At its core, it consisted of a collectively operated offset printing shop in the documenta hall, its printed products and the accompanying events. Outliving the exhibition event, Lumbung Press has now moved to Barcelona, where it currently operates.

*Where is the Art? was an internal research and discussion group for Lumbung members initiated by the Artistic Team of documenta fifteen during the preliminary research stage in 2021. Different collectives and group members could sign up according to their interests. Periodic sessions and discussions were held and harvested for a number of months, bringing Lumbung members together and allowing them to initiate discussions. The title of the group is somewhat parodical: it refers to an experience which is shared by most Lumbung members, where, because of the collective, and often immaterial nature of their practices, their artistic value is routinely questioned. The conversations generated a very fertile ground for contextualising the different artistic practices and began to generate exchanges where we could think about these questions together.
